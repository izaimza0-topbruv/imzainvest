Index Funds vs ETFs

They're basically the same thing. Here's the tiny difference.

If you've started looking into investing, you've probably seen people argue about "index funds vs ETFs". Good news: they're almost identical. Here's what actually matters.

üéØ The Short Answer

Both track a basket of stocks (like the FTSE 100 or S&P 500). The only real difference is how you buy them. Index funds are bought once per day; ETFs trade like shares throughout the day. For most beginners? It doesn't matter. Pick whichever is cheaper on your platform.

What Are They, Actually?

üìä Index Fund

A mutual fund that tracks an index (like the FTSE 100). You buy it directly from the fund company.

  • Priced once per day
  • Buy/sell at end-of-day price
  • Example: Vanguard FTSE Global All Cap Index Fund

üìà ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

An index fund that trades on the stock exchange like a regular share.

  • Priced constantly throughout the day
  • Buy/sell any time markets are open
  • Example: Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF (VWRL)

The Real Differences

Index Fund ETF
How you buy From fund company On stock exchange
Trading Once per day (4pm) Any time markets open
Price Fixed at day end Fluctuates all day
Minimum Usually ¬£1 or ¬£100/month Price of 1 share (or fractional)
Trading fees Usually none Depends on broker
Holdings Published monthly Published daily

That's it. Those are the differences. Underneath, they often hold exactly the same investments.

üìå Real Example

Vanguard offers both:

  • Index Fund: FTSE Global All Cap Index Fund ‚Äî 0.23% fee
  • ETF: FTSE All-World ETF (VWRL) ‚Äî 0.22% fee

Both track global stock markets. Both are from Vanguard. The ETF is 0.01% cheaper. That's the kind of difference we're talking about.

Which Should You Choose?

‚úÖ Go with an Index Fund if:

  • You invest monthly (set it and forget it)
  • You don't want to think about trading
  • Your platform offers them with no fees
  • You're using Vanguard directly

‚úÖ Go with an ETF if:

  • You want to buy/sell at specific times
  • Your platform has commission-free ETFs
  • You want fractional shares (Trading 212)
  • You want to see real-time prices

ü§∑ Honestly?

For Zo's portfolio, we use the Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF (VWRL) because:

  • Trading 212 offers it
  • Zero trading fees
  • Fractional shares (we can buy exactly ¬£250 worth)
  • Low fee (0.22%)

But if we were using Vanguard directly, we'd probably use their index fund version. The difference is that small.

What About "Active" Funds?

Index funds and ETFs are passive ‚Äî they just track an index. No one is picking stocks.

Active funds have a fund manager trying to beat the market. Sounds good, but:

  • Most active funds underperform the market over 10+ years
  • They charge higher fees (0.5-1.5% vs 0.1-0.3%)
  • Those fees compound against you

Our take: For most people, passive index funds/ETFs are the smarter choice. Let the market do the work.

Quick FAQ

Are ETFs riskier than index funds?

No. They carry the same investment risk (both track the same underlying stocks). The only difference is how they're traded.

Can I have both in my portfolio?

Yes. Some people use index funds for core holdings and ETFs for specific sectors. But for beginners, one global index fund/ETF is enough.

Do ETFs pay dividends?

Yes. Most ETFs either pay out dividends (distributing) or reinvest them automatically (accumulating). Accumulating is easier ‚Äî no need to reinvest yourself.

What's an accumulating ETF?

An ETF that automatically reinvests dividends. VWRL is distributing (pays out), but Vanguard also offers accumulating versions like VWCE. For long-term investing, accumulating is slightly more tax-efficient and hands-off.

UK Platforms: What Beginners Actually Use

In the UK, beginners often compare platforms like Vanguard Investor and Trading 212. Vanguard is simple and focused; Trading 212 offers wider choice and app-first experience. Neither is universally "best". The right choice depends on behaviour. If a platform makes you panic-trade, it's expensive even when fees look low.

For a new investor, we care about four things: annual account/platform charges, fund ongoing costs (OCF), dealing/spread costs, and whether you can automate monthly investing. Small fee differences compound, but emotional mistakes can cost even more.

Cost Comparison Example (Simple Numbers)

Suppose you invest ¬£5,000 initially and add ¬£100 per month for 10 years. A total fee difference of just 0.30% per year may not look dramatic in one year, but over a decade it can mean hundreds of pounds less in your account. That's real money for a teen investor.

Index funds and ETFs can both be low-cost. ETFs might look cheaper on OCF, but if you trade frequently you can lose that advantage through spread and behaviour. Index funds with monthly auto-invest can be boring in the best possible way.

ISA Wrappers: Don't Ignore Tax

For UK beginners, the tax wrapper often matters more than the product label. Holding either ETFs or index funds inside an ISA can shield gains and dividends from UK tax. We treat this as step one before debating tiny product differences.

Teen investors should understand which account they are using (Junior ISA now, adult ISA later) and what the annual limits and access rules are.

Which Is Better for Beginners?

If you're new, have limited time, and want to build habit: a broad index fund with automatic monthly investing is usually easier to stick with. If you want intraday flexibility and understand spreads and order types, ETFs can work well. The key is to choose a setup you can follow for years, not weeks.

FAQ: ETFs vs Index Funds

Can I hold both?

Yes. Many investors blend both depending on platform and strategy.

Does lower OCF always mean better?

No. Tracking quality, spreads, tax wrapper, and your behaviour matter too.

What should a 15-year-old focus on first?

Learning consistency, risk, and long-term thinking before trying to optimise tiny fee differences.
