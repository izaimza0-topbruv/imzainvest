ISA Allowance 2026: Use It or Lose It Before April 5

The clock is ticking. Whether you've been diligently saving all tax year or you've procrastinated, 5 April 2026 is your final deadline to make the most of your ¬£20,000 ISA allowance for 2025/26.

The clock is ticking. Whether you've been diligently saving all tax year or you've procrastinated, 5 April 2026 is your final deadline to make the most of your ¬£20,000 ISA allowance for 2025/26.

But here's the thing: many people don't understand how the ISA allowance works. They think they can carry over unused funds to next year. They get confused about how to split the allowance across different ISA types. And they miss out on thousands of pounds worth of potential tax-free growth.

This guide clears up the confusion and shows you exactly how to maximise your ISA allowance before the deadline.

The Golden Rule: Use It or Lose It

Your annual ISA allowance is ¬£20,000 per tax year. The tax year runs from 6 April to 5 April the following year.

Here's the critical part: any unused allowance disappears at midnight on 5 April. It doesn't roll over to next year. It doesn't accumulate. It's gone forever.

Example: If it's 5 April 2026 and you've only saved ¬£15,000 to your ISA, that remaining ¬£5,000 allowance evaporates. Next year, you'll start fresh with a new ¬£20,000 allowance‚Äîbut you've permanently lost the opportunity to save and grow that ¬£5,000 tax-free.

Over a lifetime, this adds up to tens of thousands in lost tax breaks.

Your 2025/26 ISA Allowance: ¬£20,000 Total

Let's be clear on what ¬£20,000 actually means.

You have ¬£20,000 to split however you like across:

  • Stocks & Shares ISA (investment account)
  • Cash ISA (savings account)
  • Lifetime ISA (capped at ¬£4,000 contribution, but only available to ages 18-39)
  • Innovative Finance ISA (peer-to-peer lending; less common)

Once you've used your ¬£20,000, you're done for the year. You can't contribute to any ISA type for the remainder of the 2025/26 tax year.

Key Example

You could allocate your ¬£20,000 as:

  • ¬£5,000 in a Cash ISA
  • ¬£4,000 in a Lifetime ISA (plus ¬£1,000 government bonus)
  • ¬£11,000 in a Stocks & Shares ISA
  • Total: ¬£20,000

Or this way:

  • ¬£10,000 in Stocks & Shares
  • ¬£10,000 in Cash ISA
  • Total: ¬£20,000

The split is entirely up to you‚Äîbut you can't exceed ¬£20,000 total.

Types of ISA: Which Is Right for You?

1. Stocks & Shares ISA

What it is: An investment account where your money buys stocks, funds, bonds, and ETFs. Growth is completely tax-free.

When to choose it:

  • You have 5+ years until you need the money
  • You can tolerate investment fluctuations
  • You want maximum long-term growth potential

Pros:

  • Potential for 6-10%+ annual returns (historically)
  • Flexibility to withdraw anytime with no tax penalty
  • Huge choice of investments
  • Compound growth is tax-free

Cons:

  • Value goes up and down
  • You could get back less than you invest
  • Requires some investment knowledge
  • Trades take time to settle

Expected return: 5-8% annually over 10+ years (historical average for diversified portfolios)

2. Cash ISA

What it is: A savings account earning interest, with no tax on the interest earned.

When to choose it:

  • You want guaranteed returns
  • You need access to the money within 1-2 years
  • You prefer simplicity and certainty
  • You're nervous about investment volatility

Pros:

  • Capital is protected
  • Interest is guaranteed
  • Easy to understand
  • Can withdraw anytime

Cons:

  • Interest rates are low (typically 2-4%)
  • Won't outpace inflation over the long term
  • Money does nothing‚Äîcould miss out on growth
  • Rates change, so returns aren't locked in

Current rate: Around 3-4% depending on provider (as of Feb 2026)

3. Lifetime ISA

What it is: A hybrid savings/investment account specifically for first-time homebuyers or retirement. The government adds a 25% bonus (up to ¬£1,000/year).

Eligibility:

  • Ages 18-39 only
  • British resident
  • Haven't owned a property in the past 12 months (for the home purchase bonus)

When to choose it:

  • You're a first-time buyer saving for a house deposit
  • You want a government bonus on top of your savings
  • You're under 40

Pros:

  • ¬£1 free bonus per ¬£4 you save (25% return)
  • Can use for house purchase or retirement
  • Tax-free growth
  • Up to age 50 to withdraw

Cons:

  • Locked until age 60 (or first home purchase) or 25% penalty
  • Max ¬£4,000/year contribution (only gets you ¬£1,000 bonus)
  • Complicated withdrawal rules
  • Must declare intent to use for house/retirement

How it works: Contribute ¬£4,000 ‚Üí government adds ¬£1,000 for free. That's an instant 25% return before any investment growth.

4. Innovative Finance ISA

What it is: Peer-to-peer lending wrapped in an ISA. Your money is lent to businesses or individuals, earning interest tax-free.

When to choose it:

  • You want alternatives to stocks or savings accounts
  • You're comfortable with medium risk
  • You want 3-6% returns

Pros:

  • Can offer 4-6% returns
  • Diversification from stocks/bonds
  • Tax-free income

Cons:

  • Default risk (borrower may not repay)
  • Less liquid (harder to withdraw quickly)
  • Platforms can collapse (see Funding Circle, which recently wound down)
  • Needs research into platform quality

Note: Innovative Finance ISAs are less popular now due to platform closures. Proceed with caution.

The April 5 Deadline: Why It Matters

The ISA tax year ends at midnight on 5 April. Here's why that deadline is unforgiving:

1. Banks and platforms are slammed in late March/early April. Processing delays are common. If you transfer money on 4 April, it might not reach your ISA account until 6 April‚Äîtoo late.

2. No grace period. The government doesn't care if you intended to save but the bank was slow. If the money isn't in the ISA by midnight on 5 April, it doesn't count toward your allowance.

3. Unused allowance evaporates instantly. At 00:01 on 6 April, a new tax year starts and your new ¬£20,000 allowance begins. You can't access the old year's unused allowance.

The solution: Contribute at least 2-3 weeks before 5 April to account for bank delays.

Common Misconceptions About ISA Allowances

Misconception 1: "I can carry over unused allowance to next year"

FALSE. Unused allowance is gone forever. If you don't use your ¬£20,000 by 5 April, the remainder vanishes. Next year, you get a fresh ¬£20,000.

Misconception 2: "I can have multiple ISAs of the same type"

FALSE. You can only have one active Stocks & Shares ISA and one active Cash ISA at any given time. You can't have two Trading 212 accounts, for example.

You can transfer ISAs between providers (e.g., move from Hargreaves Lansdown to Vanguard), but you can't hold two simultaneously.

Misconception 3: "My partner's ISA uses up part of my allowance"

FALSE. Each person has their own separate ¬£20,000 allowance. Your spouse, partner, and children all have their own allowances. If you're married, you have ¬£40,000 combined ISA capacity per year (¬£20,000 each).

Misconception 4: "I have to put all ¬£20,000 in one type of ISA"

FALSE. You can split it however you like:

  • ¬£10,000 Stocks & Shares + ¬£10,000 Cash ‚úì
  • ¬£15,000 S&S + ¬£4,000 Lifetime + ¬£1,000 Cash ‚úì
  • ¬£20,000 all in Stocks & Shares ‚úì
  • ¬£5,000 Innovative Finance + ¬£15,000 S&S ‚úì

Mix and match as you see fit.

Misconception 5: "I lose my ISA allowance if I don't use it all"

TRUE. This one isn't a misconception‚Äîit's the real rule. Unused allowance is genuinely lost. This is why the April 5 deadline matters so much.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: Can I withdraw money from my ISA and re-invest it in the same year?

A: No, not toward your allowance. If you withdraw ¬£5,000 from your ISA, that withdrawal counts against your ¬£20,000 allowance. You can't re-deposit it without using fresh contribution space.

However, transfers between ISA providers don't count against your allowance.

Q2: What if I open multiple ISA accounts and exceed ¬£20,000?

A: HMRC will penalise you. Tax relief is withdrawn on the excess amount, and you may face penalties. Stick to one ISA of each type, and track your total contributions.

Q3: What happens to my ISA on 6 April 2026?

A: Your existing ISA balance remains invested and tax-free. You just get a fresh ¬£20,000 allowance to contribute toward from 6 April. The ISA continues seamlessly‚Äîno disruption.

Q4: Can I contribute to a new ISA after 5 April?

A: Yes, you get a fresh ¬£20,000 allowance starting 6 April. But you can't "catch up" and contribute to the previous year's allowance.

Q5: Do I pay tax on ISA interest/growth?

A: No. That's the entire point. Interest, dividends, and capital gains are all tax-free within an ISA. You'll never pay income tax or capital gains tax on ISA returns.

The Bottom Line

Your ¬£20,000 ISA allowance for 2025/26 is a finite, non-renewable gift. Whether you earn ¬£25,000 or ¬£250,000, you get the same ¬£20,000. And every penny that goes unused is lost forever.

The April 5 deadline isn't flexible. The government doesn't grant extensions. So if you've been putting it off, now is the time to act.

You don't need to be perfect‚Äîjust decisive.

Open an account. Contribute what you can. Invest in a Stocks & Shares ISA for long-term growth, a Cash ISA for safety, or a mix of both. And do it before midnight on 5 April 2026.

Your future self will thank you.
